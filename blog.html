Phase 0 Unit 2 Week 4

May 23rd, 2015

As a programming newbie it has only recently dawned on me how useful a language can be. Or even just an aspect of a language. This week, we found out how to make Classes in Ruby and for the first time I started to get an idea of how a full program might look. It was exciting.

We've been studying enumerable methods. I had no idea what that meant until this week. It's a set of methods that do something to each element in a data set. Whether that set is a range of integers, an array of strings or a hash, an enumerable is simply a iteration over that data set to perform some function.

I had occasion this week to use the #map method. The #map method is very nice. I was attempting to change every integer within an array of both integers and strings. So I used the #map method to go through every single element to see if it was an integer or a string and if it was an integer, I'd update it.

Here's what that might look like:

source.map! do |i| if i.is_a? Numeric i + 1 else i end

Ahh, but "what's with that exclamation point?" you ask? I'll get to that.

#Map always returns an array. It can also only be used on arrays. It always goes through each element of a data set and performs some data block on it. However, #map! can actually change the original array to update it. It's a built-in function of Ruby that allows you to make changes in-line, but this one in particular only works on arrays.

So in summary: the #map method helps you to do something to every member in a data collection. You could do this if you needed to add two to every number in a set, or every fifth letter needed to be capitalized. Anyway, I think it's cool. Let me know if I've gotten anything wrong!

Phase 0 Unit 2 Week 4

May 24th, 2015

I was asked to research and discuss a problem facing the tech community and I had to tell a story about women. Multiple women. Including me. Including my mother. Including friends I had in college. I don't need to do research to tell these stories. I just have to pay attention.

I attended a very liberal, very small, Quaker college in Indiana called Earlham. It was wonderful. Full of big ideas, political correctness, and everyone wanting to save the world in their own way. A friend of mine, we'll call her Hannah, was an English major, like me, but she hadn't started there. She told me she started in the Computer Science department, but during her intro class she felt SO uncomfortable. She was the only woman in the class and all the other students and the professor were really awkward about it. The way she told it it wasn't that they openly DID anything, but it'd be... lingering looks at her breasts, openly scratching themselves, pointing out her gender for no reason. She changed her major to English because it just didn't have the same offensive culture that the Computer Science major did. She wasn't the only person I heard this from. My boyfriend at the time was in the Computer Science major and he knew exactly the culture she was talking about. He complained about it too - it was why he didn't hang out with the other CS majors. Here, in this haven of liberal thinking, where everyone's voice is valid, there remains a pocket of sexism reserved only for the Tech kids? Hannah's story and my boyfriend's discomfort told me a lot about tech culture at the time (this was 2004).

I've always been around technology. My father worked for an electronics store for as I can remember and growing up we often had beta tests of the latest versions of Windows. We had more games than we could count for multiple systems. Our home-built computers would often be upgraded with more RAM or better graphics cards. Somehow, though, I felt like technology - software and hardware - were in some way not FOR me. Perhaps it's because I only knew men who really got computers and how to manipulate them. I knew way better than mother how to manage and work with my computer, but not as well as my guy friends who knew that stuff. I took a class in high school in Visual Basic where I did just as well as the best students in the class (boys, of course) and I wasn't asked to help tutor the rest of the class while the boys were. I didn't think anything of it at the time, I was happy to have free time to make my silly little programs do silly little things and amuse myself. Looking back though, it's not cool.

The lack of women in tech is a broad, wide-reaching problem that I do believe is slowly changing. But it is filled with stories of micro-aggressions perpetrated toward individual women. And when that turns one woman off, another woman says "oh, that stuff isn't for me". Whether they don't think it's for them because it's beyond their mental reach (I can't tell you how many women I've told I'm studying web development and they look at me like I've decided to study quantum physics), or because they've never seen a woman do it, they don't even consider it an option.

Since entering this program, and getting interested in this career path, I've realized that the thing that kept me from it before was fear. Fear that I really wasn't as smart as my male friends who all knew how to write cool games and build computers. Fear that I'd experience the same kind of microaggressions that Hannah did.Until a woman sent me a link to Codecademy and two of my best female friends were attending coding bootcamps and I attended a Girl Develop It meeting, I didn't really feel like I had a place in tech. Now, those feelings are starting to change. I hope they continue. I hope they continue for all women. Because we each have a unique voice and it's worth listening to no matter who you are.

Phase 0 Unit 1 Week 3

May 17th, 2015

In my (admittedly limited) exploration of the Ruby language, it has become increasingly important to clarify the difference and significance of both Arrays and Hashes. They are two very different ways of storing information.

Arrays are for storing lists. An array might store all the names on a basketball team. An array might store several multiples of five. Or all the appetizers on the Sunday menu. It can be a collection of anything (including more arrays), but in essence it is a list. In Ruby specifically, arrays are noted in this format:

Array = ["item1", 2, "three", 4 ]

Notice that the items in an array do not need to be in any specific format. In order for many methods to be performed on an array, they will need to be in the same format, but an array needn't have any uniformity by itself.

You can also find things within an Array. If you ask the program to find the fifth thing in the array, it will do so, but the numbering is a little odd. It actually begins at 0 and continues on. So technically if you want to find the fifth thing, you must specify the fourth since the first was counted as zero. This feature is called the "index" of the array.

Hashes, however, are very different. Hashes store things in pairs called "Key: Value" pairs. An example of a hash might be a set of novels and their authors. Or perhaps a set of pizza places and your favorite pizzas at each. It could also be a set of personal information about a client. It has many uses, but it is not in any necessary order, nor is it searchable by anything other than it's keys or values.

In Ruby, a hash looks like this:
    My_info = { name: "Blanche",
    username: "Alita42",
    age: 30,
    blog: "I Made Satan Cry",
      }

So you could search this by the key "age" which would then return the integer 30. You could also search value "I Made Satan Cry" and get the key "blog".

Anyway, these things are very useful for I'm sure a myriad of things but I must confess that I am well out of time for the evening. I hope that this information was helpful for you.

May 17th, 2015

When I took the Gregorc Thinking Styles quiz, I found out I had an "Abstract Random" thinking style and I thought "Well that explains that."Not that I had any idea what it means, of course. I had to go look that up. But the phrase "Abstract Random" just seemed to sum me up.

When I looked it up I found several statements that seemed to describe me. "Dislikes routine and orderliness." "Is stretched by attending to details and giving exact answers." "Tends to prefer a noncompetitive atmosphere." These are all true statements about me.

The term "abstract random" comes from a combination of what the Gregorc theory calls a perception and an ordering ability. My strongest perception style being abstract and my strongest ordering ability being random. This means that I perceive by using hidding meandings and connections. I like to consider or imagine things I may not be able to see. This is all evidenced by my interests in mythology and symbolism. Also probably encouraged by my insistence upon reading sci-fi and fantasy novels.

The "random" ordering ability is a little harder for me to understand. The literature characterizes this as organizing information in chunks, and being able to start a project somewhere in the middle and produce the same desired results. I mean, I guess the chunks thing is fairly accurate. I definitely learn best in short spurts - like drinking bubble tea. Most of the time it's the tea, but sometimes I get this whole chunk of tapioca and I just GET IT, whatever IT is. I guess I can see now where this "random" thing is coming from.

We were also asked to reflect on Carol Dweck's fixed and growth mindsets. It's funny, in my old life I remember asking Dr. Carol Dweck to peer review one of the faculty members in the Psych department I used to work for. I think she also came to give a talk at our department.

Anyway, I feel that I've struggeled a lot with a growth vs. fixed mindset. When I read more of Dweck's work on the subject it really seemed like I was much more in the growth mindset. However, my biggest struggles have been from being fixed. I really don't handle change well. It takes me a long time to feel comfortable in a new situation. In general, I DO avoid challenges and I do give up easily.

But these were all things I intended to try to change about myself by signing up for Dev Bootcamp. I want to be better at adapting and accepting challenges. I want to DO something and not give myself the option of giving up if it got too hard. I want to be the me with a growth mindset who CAN do.

To that end, I think keeping both my "Abstract Random" learning/thinking style in mind along with a growth mindset will help me. I must remember that I need to slow down a little and to accept that I'm going to need to order information into little tapioca balls in the straw of mind (take THAT James Joyce). And my random symbolic connections are to be accepted, not fought against. I also need to remember that change is most often good. If not immediately then eventually. I think I can manage all that.

May 10th, 2015

When writing HTML code it is often useful to have two of the same element look and act differently. We can change their behavior in CSS, but the stylesheet needs to know which element you want to behave in which way. Thus, you give the two elements different attributes you can call in the stylesheet. There are two different attributes you can use for this. They are id and class.

Ids are best used as a way to pinpoint a single individual element. The attributes given to it in the stylesheet will only be applied to that specific element.

example: If you want all the text in one section to be centered, but not anywhere else, this is the code you would use.

"html:
" css: #example { text-align:center }"

If you would like several elements to behave similarly, you would use the class attribute. This way, you would only have to call the attribute once in the stylesheet and have all of your changes apply to all of the selected elements.

example: If you want all of your sections to have a grey background, this is the code you would use:

html:
css: .section { background-color: grey }

Though technically, you could use these interchangeably, these are the best practices associated with each attribute. It saves code, time and effort. In order to not be defining every element individually, you would use the class attribute. If you only need one element to behave a specific way, you should use the id attribute. The name in quotes need not be anything in particular, just something you may remember and won't be confused for some other html or css command. You could give them all names of dead Presidents, and they would work just as well.

Phase 0 Unit 1 Week 1

May 3rd, 2015

When I found out what version control was, I thought it was the coolest thing ever. Writing papers and creative writing pieces in college (it was my minor), I would have several different versions of the same file, each with a different filename (often something like Shakespeare Paper 1, Shakespeare Paper 2), and I could never easily tell what the difference was between them. If I had been able to use Git to control those versions, my life would have been much easier. Every time you commmit changes to a file, git asks you to make a commit message, summarizing the changes made. All the changes are named and packaged with the file so that you can easily navigate between versions and track edits made. This is particularly beneficial for programming. It makes it easy to remember what features you added when and track any potential bugs.

With Github, you can do this on a globally cooperative scale. I often wondered how open source software worked because in my office we couldn't have two people editing a file at the same time or else it would override all changes the other had made. This always seemed like an unavoidable problem, something we all just had to work around. But when I read about Github, I was so impressed! Finally, a way for people to be working on the same file at the same time without the danger of overriding others' work. Github's complex but necessary system of ensuring that no unnecessary changes get made to the master file is great. It will store a master version for you, and you can use branches to ensure that any changes made don't effect the master file until you are ready. It's an amazingly powerful service.

May 3, 2015

This video made me even more excited to be a part of the DBC experience. I already had an idea of the what Karim calls the Kitchen mindset before, but I think he really made it clear what the difference is between feeling like you are a part of something bigger vs. feeling like you are a consumer of goods. I have always responded well to an "all in it together" mindset. It's always been my favorite part of a job or a project. I loved participating in the theater program in my high school because it truly felt like we were all in it together and everyone must do what they can to make the production the best it can be. I have realized lately that the problems in my education have stemmed from a lack of this mindset. Not just in lecture classes where the expectation is to consume what you ordered from the menu and move along, but in small discussion courses too. The teacher may not be serving us knowledge in that case, but the self- stratification of fellow students made it difficult to feel an comeraderie.

This brings in the other point Karim makes about the two fears of people coming into Dev Bootcamp. They are either afraid of not performing as well as the other students and dragging others down, or performing much better than the other students and dragging others along. I was keenly aware of being in the first camp in college, which was also a self-fulfilling prophecy. It is exciting to know that DBC is a culture which understands and allows for both fears. I feel like the problems in a college discussion course of students stratifying themselves in this way is the responsibility of the teacher to curtail that behavior and make sure that everyone is equal. I am excited to hear that DBC values this equality so highly and works to fight against both the imposter syndrome and a destructive overconfidence.

I am so excited to spend the next few months working with people who share these values and strive for these ideals. I will confess, it makes me nervous too. I have not always been very effective at knowing exactly when imposter syndrome comes into play in my life, nor when I slip into a consumer mindset. I feel confident, though, that DBC will help me be more aware of these things and better able to change them.
